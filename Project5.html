Epidemiological Modeling

Executive Summary

Poor mental health remains a growing public health concern in the United States, yet the operational role of Complementary and Alternative Medicine (CAM) in supporting mental wellness remains under-researched. This study establishes a survey-weighted evaluation framework using the 2012 National Health Interview Survey (NHIS) to examine how CAM use over a 12-month window relates to clinical mental health outcomes among U.S. adults. The goal is to determine whether these modalities function as accessible, non-conventional coping strategies that correlate with reduced psychological distress outside traditional clinical care settings.

Data Methodology & Survey-Weighted Operationalization

To ensure population-level representation, the analysis utilized the complex survey design variables of the NHIS, applying primary sampling units (PSUs), stratification, and post-stratification weights. The dataset was structured around specific operational definitions:

  • Dependent Variables (Poor Mental Health Classification): Poor mental health was operationalized as a binary flag defined by a high Kessler Pregnancy & Mental Health psychological distress score (K6 ≥ 13) OR an affirmative response to taking prescription medication for acute anxiety or depression.
  • Independent Variables (CAM Modalities): Evaluated five distinct structural classifications of CAM usage: Chiropractic Care, Diet-Based CAM, Yoga/Mind-Body Practices, Homeopathy, and Energy Healing.
  • Statistical Framework: Developed survey-weighted Logistic Regression models to estimate odds ratios (ORs) for binary classification, paired with a Quasi-Poisson Regression pipeline to model overall K6 distress scores, effectively managing the right-skewed, count-based nature of psychological distress tracking.

Analytical Deep Dive & Predictive Modeling Results

1. Binary Classification & Single-Use Predicted Probabilities

Survey-weighted logistic regression models demonstrated a stark, statistically significant shift in the likelihood of poor mental health outcomes between non-users and single-use participants. Holding key socioeconomic covariates constant, the marginal effects show an intense protective profile even at the baseline single-use intercept:

CAM Exposure Level Predicted Probability of Poor Mental Health Standard Error (SE) Clinical Operational Context
No CAM Engagement (CAM = 0) 0.8361 0.0024 Baseline cohort meeting criteria for severe distress/medication dependency.
Single CAM Engagement (CAM = 1) 0.0202 0.0058 A dramatic reduction in predicted probability among individuals with baseline usage.

2. High-Frequency Exposure Dynamics

Time-series and frequency-response modeling demonstrated a continuous dose-response trend. Increased frequency of CAM engagement consistently corresponded with compounding protective benefits. For instance, each additional discrete unit increase in diet-based CAM usage was linked to a ~27% decrease in the odds of qualifying for the poor mental health cohort, a pattern structurally mirrored across homeopathy, chiropractic, and yoga/mind-body clusters.

3. Distress Severity Modeling: Quasi-Poisson Regression

To audit full-scale psychological distress behavior without losing information to binary thresholds, K6 count scores were evaluated using a Quasi-Poisson framework. Every analyzed CAM modality emerged as a statistically significant predictor of lower psychological distress scores, with Incidence Rate Ratios (IRR) demonstrating robust control after adjusting for Age, Sex, Education (EDU), and Income:

Methodological Takeaway: The model isolates the strongest protective correlation (lowest relative distress incidence) within the Chiropractic, Diet-Based, and Yoga/Mind-Body usage cohorts. Socioeconomic parameters scale predictably, but the persistence of robust CAM coefficients establishes their value as meaningful signals within the broader environmental model.

Public Health & Clinical Integration Recommendations

While cross-sectional survey data cannot establish direct causal mechanisms, these highly consistent, adjusted statistical associations suggest actionable pathways for clinical public health frameworks:

  1. Incorporate Holistic Modalities into Patient Intake Profiles: Given that a vast majority of patients engage with CAM independently without communicating with primary clinicians, healthcare systems should normalize the tracking of non-conventional coping mechanisms within electronic health records (EHR) to catch hidden behavioral markers.
  2. Evidence-Based Guidance for Integrative Care: Public health agencies can leverage these distinct protective patterns—particularly the high-performing profiles of mind-body practices and dietary tracking—to build out validated, integrative treatment guidelines that supplement standard pharmaceutical or psychiatric protocols.
  3. Targeted Resource Allocation for Low-Barrier Wellness: Because even single-use engagement corresponds with drastically lower probability brackets of severe mental distress, community wellness initiatives should focus on scaling public access to foundational mind-body education programs to serve as proactive, low-cost preventative healthcare buffers.
